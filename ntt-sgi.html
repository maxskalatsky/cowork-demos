The SGI Calculations — A Plain-English Review

Six things worth a second look before we build them, explained simply, with examples.

Prepared for  Crosshand × NTT Topic  “NTT - SGI” spec Date  June 26, 2026 Draft v1 · For review

The 30-second version

The arithmetic is correct. Every formula in the document adds up exactly as written. We are not saying anyone did the math wrong.

Our questions are about design choices — a few places where the rules either don’t match how the game is actually scored in the app, or where the result won’t behave the way we think the teams expect. Each one below has a simple explanation, a real example, and a decision for us to make together.

Bottom line: none of these are hard to fix. We just want to agree on the answers before we write code, because some of them affect how scores are stored and scaled — and that’s expensive to change later.

First, how it’s supposed to work

In one breath, so the rest makes sense.

Every time a player takes a turn (a chip and maybe a putt), we record what happened. We add those turns up into four “report-card” scores, average them into one number called the SGI, and use the gap between two players’ SGIs to decide if the weaker player should get a few free points (a handicap) to keep the match fair.

Each turn→ 4 report-card scores→ SGI (0–100)→ Handicap for the weaker player

The four report-card scores are: Up & Down (did you finish in 2 shots?), Circle Accuracy (did your chip land in the close zone?), One-Putt (did you sink your putt?), and Scoring Efficiency (points you earned vs. the most you could).

Problem 1. The fairness helper almost never turns on

1

For normal players the handicap rounds down to zero High

Imagine this Imagine a “fairness helper” at a basketball hoop that lowers the rim for the shorter kid — but it only ever moves if one kid is a toddler. Two regular kids of slightly different height? It does nothing. So most of the time, it might as well not be there.
In golf terms The handicap is built on 9 ÷ SGI, which is a “reciprocal.” The result is that the gap between two good players is tiny, so it rounds away to nothing. The helper only wakes up when one player is a near-beginner.
MatchupSGI vs SGIHandicap given
Two strong players55 vs 650 (scratch)
Average gap40 vs 600 (scratch)
Big gap20 vs 602 points
The spec’s own example6 vs 585 points (capped)
Note the example pairs an SGI of 6 (almost the worst score possible) with an average 58. That’s the only kind of matchup where the helper does much.
Why it matters
If most of our players land between, say, 40 and 70, almost every match will be “scratch” and the handicap will rarely change anything. Decision: is that intended? If we want closer matches to also get a nudge, here are three ways to fix it.
Options to consider · pick one to discuss
A
Use the SGI gap directly (simplest, most predictable)Suggested start Drop the 9 ÷ SGI step and compute straight from the difference: Handicap = (better SGI − weaker SGI) ÷ 8, capped at 5. A 10-point gap always means the same nudge, whether the players are great or weak. Example: 40 vs 60 → 20 ÷ 8 = 2.5 → 2–3 points (it’s 0 today).
B
Keep the current formula, just turn up the dial Smallest change — keep Crosshand’s 9 ÷ SGI shape but raise the multiplier (e.g. swap “× 10 ÷ 2” for roughly “× 20”) so typical gaps land at 1–3 points instead of rounding to zero. Example: 40 vs 60 → about 2 points; a 6-vs-58 blowout still hits the cap of 5.
C
Use a simple lookup table Skip the formula and map gaps to points by hand: 0–5 → 0, 6–15 → 1, 16–25 → 2, 26–35 → 3… up to the cap. Totally controllable and easy to explain, at the cost of being hand-tuned rather than calculated.
D
Decide it’s intentional and leave it Accept that only lopsided matchups get strokes and nearly everything else is scratch. No code change — but we should choose this on purpose, not by accident.

Problem 2. “Scoring Efficiency” can go over 100% (or never reach it)

2

The “most points possible” doesn’t match how the game actually scores High

Imagine this Imagine a test that’s “out of 10,” but one surprise bonus question is secretly worth 3 points. Ace everything and you score 11 out of 10 — 110%. The “out of 10” label was wrong, so the percentage stops making sense.
In golf terms The app actually awards 3 points for chipping straight into the hole, and at most 2 otherwise. But the spec’s example treats the most-you-can-get as if it were smaller. Three different places disagree about the maximum:
SourceSays max per turn is…
The app’s referee screen (live code)3 (a hole-out)
The data model’s own comment2
The spec’s worked example (9 ÷ 9)1
So if a player hole-outs a few times, Scoring Efficiency can read over 100%. And if we instead say the max is 3, a player who plays perfectly but never hole-outs is stuck at 67% forever.
Why it matters
A percentage that can exceed 100% — or can never reach it — will confuse players and break any ranking that assumes 0–100. Below are the ways to make the percentage behave (always 0–100).
Options to consider · pick one to discuss
A
For the rating, treat a chip-in as 2 points (not 3)Suggested start Keep the exciting 3-point hole-out in the live game, but when calculating SGI, cap a turn at 2 (a chip-in counts the same as “in the circle + putt made”). The scale stays a clean 0–2 per turn, so 100% is genuinely reachable by playing a perfect short game. Example: 9 turns, best play = 18 points = 100%.
B
Make the maximum a true hole-out (3 points) Set “maximum possible” to 3 per turn, so SEP = points ÷ (turns × 3). The percentage is honest and never tops 100%, but a player who chips close and putts perfectly — yet never holes out — is capped at 67%, because holing out is treated as the only way to be “perfect.”
C
Whichever we choose, make all three sources agree Today the referee code says max 3, the data model’s note says 2, and the spec example implies 1. Pick one canonical points-per-turn table and use the exact same one in the app, the data model, and the spec. This is needed no matter which of A or B we land on.

Problem 3. Three of the four scores measure almost the same thing

3

“Holing the ball” gets counted about three times Medium

Imagine this Imagine judging a smoothie on four things: “fruitiness,” “sweetness,” “sugar,” and “taste.” Three of those rise and fall together. So really you’re judging sweetness three times and everything else once — even though it looks like four fair categories.
In golf terms When a player sinks the ball, three of the four scores jump at once:
The moment you hole the ball……this score goes up
You finished in 2 shots or fewerUp & Down ↑
You made the puttOne-Putt ↑
You earned pointsScoring Efficiency ↑
Meanwhile, chipping it close (but not in) only nudges Circle Accuracy. So “sink it” counts roughly triple, and “great chip” counts once.
Why it matters
Calling them “four equally weighted categories” sounds balanced, but in practice the index mostly rewards putting/holing. That may be exactly what we want — or we may want to weight chipping more.
Options to consider · pick one to discuss
A
Keep all four equal — just confirm it’s on purposeSuggested start Four simple, equal categories are easy to explain to players and look balanced on a profile. The overlap isn’t fatal — it just means “finishing the hole” is what the rating rewards most, which is arguably the point of a short-game index. No code change; we just decide consciously.
B
Re-weight so chipping counts more Give Circle Accuracy (the one independent measure) a bigger share. Example: Circle Accuracy 40%, the other three 20% each. Keeps all four categories but stops putting/holing from dominating.
C
Collapse to two non-overlapping measures Replace the three holing-related scores with one “Conversion” score, and keep “Circle Accuracy” for chipping — two things that measure genuinely different skills. Cleaner statistically, but a bigger change to the player-facing breakdown.

Problem 4. Some players break the math by being too good (or too new)

4

“Divide by zero” for great chippers and brand-new players Medium

Imagine this Ask someone, “What percent of your free throws went in?” If they never took one, the honest answer isn’t 0% — there’s no answer at all. Dividing by zero just breaks.
In golf terms One-Putt % is putts made ÷ putts attempted. A player who chips the ball into the hole every time never attempts a putt — so the math becomes 0 ÷ 0, which is undefined. Ironically, the best chipper gets a broken putting stat. The same thing happens for a brand-new player who hasn’t taken a single turn — every score is 0 ÷ 0, so we can’t even produce an SGI for their first match.
Why it matters
Without a rule, these players show garbage stats or crash a calculation. We need a rule for “no data yet.”
Options to consider · these combine — decide each
A
Empty score shows “—”, never 0%Suggested If a score has nothing to divide (e.g. zero putts attempted), show a dash and leave it out of the average — don’t pretend it’s 0%. Example: a great chipper who never has to putt shows One-Putt “—”, not a misleading 0%.
B
First-timers get a starting SGISuggested Give a brand-new player a baseline (e.g. 50, the middle of the scale) so the handicap math always has two numbers to work with. We just need to agree the starting value.
C
No handicap until a player is “established” Mark players “provisional” until they’ve logged enough turns (e.g. 18–27); their early matches are played scratch. Avoids handing out strokes based on one lucky or unlucky round.

Problem 5. The example player couldn’t exist in real life

5

100% on the total but 44% on every part — impossible together Medium

Imagine this A student says, “I got 100% on the whole test… but only 44% on every single section.” That can’t be true at the same time. If every section was 44%, the total has to be about 44% too.
In golf terms The sample player in the spec has Up & Down 44%, Circle 44%, One-Putt 44% — but Scoring Efficiency 100%. Scoring Efficiency at 100% means the player earned the maximum on every turn, which would force the other three near 100% too. The numbers are fine for teaching the formula, but they don’t describe a real golfer.
Why it matters
No code problem here — just a heads-up so nobody on either team treats that 44 / 44 / 44 / 100 → SGI 58 line as a realistic player or uses it to sanity-check real data. Nothing to decide here — just tidy up the final spec.
Options to consider · housekeeping for the final spec
A
Write one realistic, consistent exampleSuggested Once Problems 2–4 are settled, build a sample player where the four scores and the SGI actually fit together (best done after those decisions, since the right numbers depend on them).
B
For now, just label it “illustrative only” Cheapest fix — add a one-line caption to the 44 / 44 / 44 / 100 example saying it shows the formula’s mechanics, not a real player.
C
Show two examples — a strong and a weak player Include one high-SGI and one low-SGI player, both internally consistent, so readers see the realistic range the rating produces.

Problem 6. The handicap uses a “9” that the game doesn’t always use

6

A measuring stick whose length changes Medium

Imagine this Imagine measuring everyone’s height with a ruler that’s sometimes 9 inches and sometimes 11 inches long, and that even shrinks while you measure. Your “height” depends on the ruler, not just on you.
In golf terms The handicap formula multiplies by a fixed Target Score = 9. But in the app the target isn’t always 9: semifinals and finals play to 11, and the target even drops by 1 on a scoreless hole. Because the handicap scales directly with that number, using 11 instead of 9 makes every handicap about 22% bigger.
Why it matters
The same two players could get a different handicap depending on the round — for a reason that has nothing to do with their skill. Three ways to settle it.
Options to consider · pick one to discuss
A
Fix the number at 9, alwaysSuggested start Use 9 in the handicap formula no matter the round, even when the round plays to 11. A player’s handicap then depends only on skill, not on which round they happen to be in. Simple and stable.
B
Follow the round’s real target on purpose Use 9 or 11 to match the round being played, accepting that higher-stakes rounds give slightly bigger strokes. Fine — as long as it’s a deliberate choice, not an accident.
C
Remove the number entirely If we adopt Option A in Problem 1 (handicap straight from the SGI gap), there’s no “÷ SGI” and no target multiplier at all — and this whole problem simply disappears. The problems are linked, so it’s worth deciding them together.

What’s already good

The structure is genuinely strong: four simple report-card scores → one number → a fairness handicap is easy to explain to players, and the referee screen already collects the right detail at the moment of play. The fixes above are about agreeing on definitions, not redesigning the system. Once we settle problems 1–2, the rest fall into place quickly.

Suggested next step: a short working session with Crosshand (formula intent) and NTT (player experience) to answer the six decisions, then we lock the spec and build.
